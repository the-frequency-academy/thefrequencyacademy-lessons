What Is the Flower of Life?

The Flower of Life is 19 equal circles arranged in six-fold symmetry, enclosed within a larger boundary circle. It is the direct continuation of the Seed of Life — same rule, same radius, same compass placement at each new intersection — extended until the pattern is complete.

But the 19 circles are not the whole picture. The pattern does not stop at the enclosing boundary. It continues in all directions, infinitely. The Flower of Life is a finite window into an infinite tessellation — a pattern that tiles the plane without gaps or overlaps, extending forever. The enclosing circle is a frame. The pattern is boundless.

The Flower Across the Ancient World

The Flower of Life has been documented at sites around the ancient world that have no known historical connection to one another. Different cultures. Different languages. Different epochs. The same 19 circles in the same arrangement. This is not coincidence — this is recognition.

Abydos, Egypt
Temple of Osiris · ~10,500 BCE
Carved into granite columns. One of the oldest known examples. The geometry is unmistakable — burned or carved into the stone with a precision that astonishes modern researchers.
Ephesus, Turkey
Temple of Artemis · ~550 BCE
Carved into the marble floor of one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. A civilisation thousands of miles and years from Egypt encoded the same pattern.
Leonardo da Vinci
Notebooks · ~1490 CE
Da Vinci drew the Flower extensively and studied its mathematical properties. His Vitruvian Man uses the same proportional system encoded in the Flower.
Forbidden City, China
Beijing · Imperial Era
Spheres held by stone lions at the entrance encode the Flower of Life pattern — the same geometry present at the threshold of the world's most powerful imperial complex.
Indian Temples
Hampi, Ajanta · ~500 CE+
The Flower appears in stone carvings across ancient Indian temple architecture spanning multiple traditions and centuries.
Phoenician Art
~1200 BCE
Among the earliest portable encodings of the form — appearing in ivory carvings from the ancient Phoenician culture of the Levant.

Quick Reference — Key Definitions

Flower of Life ›
Sacred geometry pattern of 19 equal circles in six-fold symmetry, enclosed within a larger circle. The most widely recognised symbol in sacred geometry. Appears in ancient Egypt, Greece, China, India, and Leonardo da Vinci's notebooks.
Fruit of Life ›
The 13-circle pattern within the Flower selected by taking every other intersection. Connecting all 13 centres produces Metatron's Cube — which contains all five Platonic Solids.
Tessellation ›
A tiling of the plane without gaps or overlaps. The Flower of Life circle pattern can tile infinitely — the visible 19-circle Flower is a bounded window of this infinite field. Only a handful of geometric forms possess this property.
Blueprint of Creation ›
The Flower's property of encoding all sacred forms — Seed, Fruit, Metatron's Cube, all Platonic Solids, Sri Yantra, Merkaba — within a single 19-circle template. Not metaphor: a literal description of the informational content of the pattern.
Abydos ›
Ancient Egyptian city on the Nile; site of the Temple of Osiris. Contains one of the oldest known carvings of the Flower of Life, inscribed into granite columns. The age of the carvings is debated, with some researchers dating them to over 10,000 years ago.
Vitruvian Proportion ›
The proportional system encoded in the Flower of Life and used by Leonardo da Vinci in his Vitruvian Man — the same proportions appear in the human body and in classical architecture. The Flower contains human geometry.
