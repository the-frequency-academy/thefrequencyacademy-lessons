What Is the Vesica Piscis?

The Vesica Piscis — Latin for "bladder of a fish" — is the lens-shaped region created when two circles of identical radius are placed so that the edge of each circle passes directly through the centre of the other. It is the second act of sacred geometry, arising the moment G1's singular circle is joined by its twin.

What makes this construction extraordinary is not its beauty alone — though it is undeniably beautiful — but what is encoded within it. The ratio of the vesica's width to its height is exactly 1:√3 (1 to the square root of 3, approximately 1.732). Like π embedded in the circle, this irrational number lives permanently in the most fundamental two-circle relationship possible. The infinite is written into the first act of relationship.

The Vesica Piscis is not one form among many. It is the generative womb of the entire geometric lineage — the source from which all sacred forms that follow in this module emerge.

  • Formed by two equal circles, each passing through the other's centre
  • Encodes √3 in its width-to-height ratio — an irrational, infinite number like π
  • Directly generates the equilateral triangle — the first polygon in sacred geometry
  • Is the geometric parent of every subsequent sacred form: hexagon, Star of David, Seed of Life, Flower of Life
  • Appears across Christian, Egyptian, Islamic, Celtic, and Masonic traditions as the eye, the womb, the overlap of worlds
  • The intentional drawing practice charges the geometry with meaning — geometry is both mathematics and meditation

Constructing the Vesica Piscis

The Vesica Piscis is the first geometric construction in the sacred geometry tradition. Every form that follows in this module is built on or within it.

  • 1

    Draw the First Circle

    Place your compass needle anywhere on a blank page. Open to any radius r. Draw a complete circle. Mark the centre point A.

  • 2

    Place the Second Centre

    Without changing the radius, move the compass needle to any point on the circumference of the first circle. Mark this as centre point B.

  • 3

    Draw the Second Circle

    With the needle at B, draw a second complete circle of exactly the same radius. This circle will pass directly through the centre A of the first circle.

  • 4

    Identify the Vesica

    The lens-shaped region where the circles overlap is the Vesica Piscis. There are two intersection points where the circles cross — mark these C and D.

  • 5

    Find the Triangle

    Connect A, B, and either C or D. Because AB = AC = BC = r (the radius), this is a perfect equilateral triangle. The Vesica Piscis has just generated the first polygon.

The Vesica Piscis Across Traditions

This is one of the most cross-cultural symbols in human history — appearing across at least six major wisdom traditions, each recognising the same fundamental truth encoded in two overlapping circles.

Christian Art
Mandorla / Vesica
The almond-shaped aureole surrounding Christ, the Virgin, or saints in glory. Represents the meeting of the human and divine.
Christian Symbol
Ichthys (Fish)
The outline of the Vesica Piscis used as the early Christian fish symbol. Predates and outlasts specific doctrines.
Freemasonry
The Point within Two Circles
The Vesica framing the point — symbolising the soul bounded by universal law and divine proportion.
Ancient Egypt
Eye of Horus / Ra
The almond eye shape — the Vesica Piscis as the eye of divine consciousness looking out from the eternal field.
Celtic
The Celtic Lens
The vesica appears throughout Celtic knotwork and illuminated manuscripts as a symbol of the overlap of worlds.
Islamic Geometry
Al-Mandal
The foundational lens shape used in the construction of complex Islamic geometric patterns and star polygons.

Quick Reference — Key Definitions

Vesica Piscis ›
Latin for 'bladder of a fish.' The lens-shaped region formed by the intersection of two circles of equal radius, where the edge of each circle passes through the centre of the other. The width to height ratio is 1:√3.
Intersection ›
The region where two geometric forms overlap. In the case of two equal circles positioned so each passes through the other's centre, the intersection is the Vesica Piscis — a mandorla or almond shape.
Mandorla ›
Italian for 'almond.' Another name for the Vesica Piscis shape, used in art and iconography. In Christian art, the mandorla surrounds the figure of Christ or the Virgin Mary in representations of divine transcendence.
√3 (Root 3) ›
The square root of 3, approximately 1.732. The ratio of the width of the Vesica Piscis to its height is exactly 1:√3. This irrational number — like π — cannot be expressed as a finite decimal, encoding the infinite in the most basic two-circle relationship.
Equilateral Triangle ›
A triangle with all three sides equal and all angles 60°. The equilateral triangle is the first polygon that emerges from the Vesica Piscis — its three vertices are the two circle centres and one of the two intersection points. This makes the Vesica Piscis the geometric parent of the triangle.
Ichthys ›
The ancient Christian fish symbol. Two arcs forming a fish shape — directly derived from the outline of the Vesica Piscis. The symbol predates Christianity and encodes the same sacred geometry across traditions.

Practical Application — This Week

The Vesica Piscis is not a concept to hold in the mind — it is a practice to work with the hands and inhabit with awareness. This week, bring the geometry into your physical world.

🧭
Construction Practice

Draw the Vesica Piscis with compass and straightedge following the 5 steps. Allow 15–20 minutes. Work through all four constructions: basic vesica, equilateral triangle, double triangle, and intentional drawing.

👁
Spot the Vesica

Find at least three vesica shapes in your environment this week — in architecture, logos, religious art, or nature. Photograph them. Notice what traditions or contexts they arise in.

✍️
Intentional Drawing

Draw one final Vesica Piscis with full intention. Before placing the first circle, choose what the two centres represent — two aspects of yourself, two people, two qualities in relationship. Draw slowly.

📐
Verify the Math

After constructing your Vesica, measure the width and height with a ruler. Divide height by width. You should get approximately 1.732 — the square root of 3. Verify the infinite in your own drawing.
