02
The Experiment

In 1801, Thomas Young fired light through two narrow slits and watched it land on a screen behind. Instead of two bright bands — one behind each slit — he saw a pattern of many alternating bright and dark stripes. Waves were interfering with each other. Light was behaving as a wave.

A century later, scientists repeated the experiment with single electrons — one at a time, so they could not interfere with each other. Impossibly, the interference pattern still emerged. Each electron seemed to go through both slits simultaneously, interfering with its own wave function.

Then they added a detector to observe which slit each electron used. The moment they looked — the interference pattern vanished. Observation itself had collapsed the wave into a particle.

The Matter Wave

In 1924, a French physics doctoral student named Louis de Broglie made a proposal so audacious his thesis committee didn't know what to do with it. He sent the paper to Einstein, who read it and wrote back: "This is not crazy. This is real."

De Broglie's insight: if light (a wave) can behave as particles, then particles must also behave as waves. Every piece of matter — every electron, every atom, every human body — has an associated wavelength. You are a wave function. You have a frequency.
