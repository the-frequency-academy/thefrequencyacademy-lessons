The Transmission

In classical physics, the universe was a machine. Objects existed in definite states. You could measure everything without disturbing it. The observer was irrelevant — a passive witness to a reality that existed independently of being watched.

Quantum mechanics dismantled this completely.

The Double Slit Experiment — one of the most profound experiments in the history of science — demonstrated something that still challenges every assumption we make about the nature of reality: the act of observation collapses possibility into actuality.

When no one is watching, particles behave as waves — existing in multiple states simultaneously, spreading through space as pure probability. The moment a detector is placed to observe which slit the particle passes through, the wave collapses. The particle becomes a particle. It chooses.

The observer does not discover reality. The observer participates in creating it.

This is not philosophy. This is experimental fact, replicated thousands of times in laboratories around the world. And it changes everything about what frequency work, intention, and ceremony actually are.

The Double Slit Experiment

The experiment that proved observation changes reality — described simply:

Without Observation

Particles fired one at a time through two slits create an interference pattern on the screen — as if each particle passes through both slits simultaneously as a wave.

With Observation

Place a detector to measure which slit the particle uses. Immediately the interference pattern vanishes. The particle behaves as a particle — going through one slit only.

The Impossible Part

Even if the detector is placed after the particle has already passed through — and then erased — the pattern changes retroactively. Observation affects the past.

What It Means

Reality exists as probability waves until consciousness interacts with it. The universe is not a collection of things — it is a field of potential awaiting participation.

Key Concepts

Wave Function Collapse

Before observation, a quantum system exists as a superposition of all possible states. Measurement forces it to "choose" — collapsing infinite possibility into one actuality.

The Measurement Problem

Physics cannot agree on what constitutes "observation." Does it require consciousness? A detector? Interaction? This remains one of the deepest unsolved questions in science.

Non-Locality

Quantum effects can occur instantaneously across any distance. Two entangled particles affect each other faster than light could travel between them.

Retrocausality

The Delayed Choice Quantum Eraser experiment suggests observation can affect events that have already occurred. Time may not be as fixed as we believe.
