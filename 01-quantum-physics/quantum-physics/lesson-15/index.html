The Electrical Cell — Every Cell Is a Battery

The cell membrane is not merely a barrier. It is an electrical device. The interior of a resting cell is maintained at approximately negative 70 millivolts relative to the exterior — a difference sustained continuously by protein pumps embedded in the membrane. These sodium-potassium ATPase pumps move three sodium ions out of the cell for every two potassium ions they bring in, maintaining the charge differential against the natural tendency toward equilibrium. Every pump cycle consumes one molecule of ATP — the cell's energy currency. Maintaining the membrane potential is one of the primary energy expenditures of all living cells.

The numbers are worth sitting with. A AA battery runs at 1.5 volts. A cell membrane at -70 millivolts seems comparatively modest. But the membrane is only approximately 7 nanometers thick. Calculate the electric field strength: 70 millivolts across 7 nanometers equals approximately 10 million volts per meter. For reference, the electric field inside a lightning bolt is approximately 10 million to 100 million volts per meter. Your cells maintain a field strength at the lower end of the lightning range — continuously, in every living cell, as long as you are alive.

This voltage is not passive. It is functional. Membrane potential controls which ion channels open and close, regulating what enters and leaves the cell. Nutrient uptake, waste excretion, protein synthesis, gene expression — all depend on the cell's electrical state. Cancer cells have significantly lower membrane potential than healthy cells — a measurable electrical signature of pathological state, and a proposed target for electrical cancer therapies. Healthy voltage is not incidental to healthy cells. It is the substrate of cellular function.

ESTABLISHED SCIENCE

Cell membrane potential is measured routinely in electrophysiology research using patch-clamp techniques — a Nobel Prize-winning methodology. The -70mV figure is standard in every physiology textbook. This is not controversial. The electrical nature of cells is foundational biology.

The Electrical Nervous System — 250 Miles Per Hour

When a neuron fires, the membrane potential doesn't merely change — it reverses dramatically and rapidly. The voltage shifts from -70 millivolts to approximately +30 millivolts in less than a millisecond, then returns to the resting potential. This event is called an action potential. It propagates along the axon of the neuron — the long extension through which signals travel — at velocities ranging from 0.5 meters per second in slow unmyelinated fibers to 120 meters per second (over 250 miles per hour) in myelinated motor neurons.

Your brain contains approximately 86 billion neurons, each capable of firing up to 200 times per second, each connecting to thousands of others through chemical synapses that convert electrical signals to chemical and back to electrical across the synaptic gap. The collective electrical activity of billions of neurons oscillating in coordinated patterns produces what we measure externally as brainwaves — rhythmic electrical patterns detectable through electrodes placed on the scalp in an EEG.

These brainwave frequencies are not metaphors for mental states. They are the measurable electrical signature of those states. Delta (0.5–4 Hz) occurs in deep sleep. Theta (4–8 Hz) in meditation and creativity. Alpha (8–13 Hz) in relaxed awareness. Beta (13–30 Hz) in active thinking. Gamma (30–100+ Hz) in peak cognition and insight. When external frequencies influence these patterns — through binaural beats, isochronic tones, Schumann resonance exposure, or PEMF — the mechanism is electrical: external electrical oscillations coupling with internal electrical oscillations. This is entrainment — a physical phenomenon of electrical systems, not a metaphysical one.

The Heart's Electromagnetic Field — The Strongest Signal in the Body

The heartbeat begins as an electrical event. The sinoatrial node — a cluster of cells in the right atrium — spontaneously generates an electrical impulse that spreads across the atrial muscle, passes through the atrioventricular node, travels down the bundle of His and Purkinje fibers, and triggers coordinated ventricular contraction. This electrical choreography occurs roughly 60–100 times per minute at rest for a typical adult — approximately 100,000 times per day, approximately 3.5 billion times in a lifetime. We measure it at the skin surface as the ECG/EKG.

The heart's electromagnetic field is the strongest in the body by a substantial margin. Electrically, it is approximately 100 times stronger than the brain's field. Magnetically, approximately 5,000 times stronger. This field does not remain inside the body — it radiates outward in a toroidal (donut-shaped) geometry and can be detected several feet from the body using magnetometers sensitive enough to measure it (SQUID magnetometers, used in magnetocardiography research).

Research from the HeartMath Institute has demonstrated that the pattern of this field — its coherence — changes measurably with emotional state. In states of sustained positive emotion (appreciation, gratitude, love), the heart's rhythm becomes highly ordered: a smooth, sine-wave-like pattern called cardiac coherence. In states of stress, frustration, or anxiety, the pattern becomes erratic and irregular. Because the electromagnetic field of the heart extends outside the body, this coherence — or incoherence — is not merely an internal state. It is broadcast. Other people's autonomic nervous systems can detect and synchronize with a coherent heart field in proximity. When two people are in sustained close contact, their heart rhythms show measurable synchronization.
