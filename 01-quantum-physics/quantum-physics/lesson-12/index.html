The Electromagnetic Spectrum

You know the rainbow — red, orange, yellow, green, blue, violet. But that rainbow is less than 1% of what is actually there. Below it, above it, and stretching in both directions beyond anything your eyes can detect: one phenomenon, one physics, infinite frequencies.

The Transmission

A prism takes white light and splits it apart — and suddenly you can see what was always there, hidden inside the apparent emptiness. Red bleeds into orange, into yellow, green, blue, violet. The spectrum of visible light, spread across a wall. It is one of the most beautiful things physics produces.

But here is what the prism cannot show you: below the red edge, invisible to your eye, infrared radiation continues — the heat you feel from a fire, from the sun, from your own hand held close to your face. And below that, microwaves — carrying your wireless signal and heating your food. And below that, radio waves, some of them kilometers long, carrying broadcasts across continents. The Schumann resonance — 7.83 Hz, Earth's electromagnetic heartbeat — lives at the very bottom of this range, its wavelength so long it wraps around the entire planet.

Above the violet edge, ultraviolet begins — the frequencies that tan your skin and sterilize water. Then X-rays, which pass through flesh and stop at bone. Then gamma rays, produced by nuclear reactions and cosmic events, their wavelengths smaller than the nucleus of an atom.

It is all one thing. Radio waves, visible light, gamma radiation — they are all oscillating electric and magnetic fields traveling at the speed of light. The only difference between them is frequency. Understanding where every frequency sits on this spectrum is how you develop a map of reality that extends far beyond the visible.

What Electromagnetic Radiation Actually Is

An electromagnetic wave is not a thing that travels through something. It is two oscillating fields — electric and magnetic — that propagate each other through space. An oscillating electric field generates a changing magnetic field. That changing magnetic field generates a changing electric field. The two fields sustain each other across empty space with no medium required. This is why light can travel from a star to your eye across billions of miles of vacuum.

The speed at which this propagation occurs is fixed: approximately 299,792,458 meters per second — the speed of light, c. This is not the speed of photons in some philosophical sense. It is the measured propagation velocity of electromagnetic fields in vacuum, consistent to many decimal places across all frequencies and all observers. The constancy of c is foundational to special relativity and to all of modern physics.

Because the speed is constant across all EM frequencies, and because wave speed = frequency × wavelength, frequency and wavelength are locked in an inverse relationship for all EM radiation: higher frequency means shorter wavelength, lower frequency means longer wavelength. A radio wave at 1 MHz has a wavelength of 300 meters. Visible green light at 550 THz has a wavelength of 545 nanometers. A gamma ray at 10 EHz has a wavelength smaller than an atomic nucleus.

THE ENERGY RELATIONSHIP — E = hf

Energy per photon = Planck's constant × frequency. This means higher frequency EM carries more energy per quantum. Radio wave photons carry around 10⁻²⁸ Joules each — completely incapable of ionizing biological molecules. Gamma ray photons carry around 10⁻¹² Joules each — capable of tearing electrons from atoms and breaking DNA bonds. The spectrum is not merely a catalog of frequencies. It is a spectrum of energy per quantum, with profound consequences for how each band interacts with matter.

THE KEY INSIGHT

The frequencies that affect biology most directly tend to be in the extremely low to ultra-low frequency range. This is where the brain operates, where Earth resonates, where cells communicate electrically. These frequencies carry very little energy per photon — they cannot ionize molecules or cause direct tissue damage. What they can carry is information. And information, organized as coherent frequency patterns resonant with biological systems, is what shapes biological function.

This Week's Practice — Map Your Frequency Environment

You are continuously bathed in electromagnetic radiation across multiple bands simultaneously. This week, develop awareness of what frequencies are actually present in your environment and where they sit on the spectrum.

1
Audit your immediate EM environment. In the space you inhabit most — your home, your desk, your bedroom — identify every device emitting EM radiation and use the Frequency Finder to place each one on the spectrum. Your phone (850 MHz–2.6 GHz), WiFi router (2.4 or 5 GHz), microwave oven (2.45 GHz), Bluetooth (2.4 GHz), any PEMF device, any tensor rings, any lighting (infrared through visible through UV). Write the complete list. Notice what bands are densest.
2
Find the Schumann resonance on the finder and compare it to your WiFi frequency. The Schumann fundamental is 7.83 Hz. WiFi is 2.4 billion Hz. Calculate the ratio. This ratio is approximately 300 million to 1. This is not a reason to avoid technology — it is a reason to understand that your nervous system evolved in an environment dominated by one set of frequencies and now operates in an environment with another set many orders of magnitude higher in frequency. What that means is a matter of ongoing scientific investigation.
3
Spend time in the low-frequency end of the spectrum deliberately. Go outside without devices. Bare feet on ground. No phone, no earbuds. Earth's surface emits ELF fields in the Schumann range. Your brain's dominant frequency in alert rest is alpha — 8–12 Hz, the same range as Schumann harmonics. For 20 minutes, let the dominant EM frequencies you're exposed to be the ones your nervous system evolved alongside. Notice what happens to your state.
4
Evaluate one frequency claim using the spectrum as your framework. Find a claim about a frequency being either healing or harmful — from any source. Place that frequency on the spectrum using the finder. Note its energy per photon, its wavelength, its band. Ask: is this frequency ionizing or non-ionizing? Does it resonate with biological frequencies or is it many orders of magnitude removed? What physical mechanism is proposed? The spectrum is your map for evaluating these claims with intelligence rather than either acceptance or dismissal.
